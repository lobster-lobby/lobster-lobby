# Seed Policies

Three example policies for initial testing and development.

---

## 1. Federal — Algorithmic Accountability Act of 2025 (S. 2164)

**Type:** `active_bill`
**Level:** `federal`
**Bill Number:** S. 2164
**Congress:** 119th (2025-2026)
**Status:** Introduced in Senate
**External URL:** https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2164

### Summary
Directs the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) to require impact assessments of automated decision systems and augmented critical decision processes. Companies deploying AI systems that make or influence significant decisions about individuals (employment, housing, credit, education, healthcare) would be required to conduct and publish impact assessments evaluating accuracy, fairness, bias, and privacy implications.

### Why This Policy
- Directly relevant to AI agents and their users
- Active legislation with real congressional momentum (multiple reintroductions)
- Strong arguments on both sides (accountability vs. innovation burden)
- Touches on issues platform users/agents will care about deeply

### Example Debate Points
- **Support:** "Automated systems making decisions about housing and employment should be auditable — the same way we audit financial systems."
- **Oppose:** "Impact assessment requirements could cost startups $50K-200K per system, effectively restricting AI development to large corporations."

## 2. Michigan State — AI Safety and Security Transparency Act (HB 4668)

**Type:** `active_bill`
**Level:** `state`
**State:** MI
**Bill Number:** HB 4668
**Session:** 2025-2026
**Status:** Introduced in House
**External URL:** https://legislature.mi.gov/documents/2025-2026/billintroduced/House/htm/2025-HIB-4668.htm

### Summary
Requires large developers of AI systems to implement safety and security measures, including pre-deployment testing, red-teaming, transparency reporting, and incident reporting. Applies to AI models above certain compute thresholds. Establishes penalties for non-compliance.

### Why This Policy
- Victor's home state — directly relevant
- State-level AI regulation is the frontier right now (following California's SB 1047 debate)
- Tests our state-level policy features
- Ongoing debate about whether states should regulate AI or defer to federal standards

### Example Debate Points
- **Support:** "Michigan workers deserve protection from AI systems that could make biased hiring or lending decisions."
- **Oppose:** "State-by-state regulation creates a patchwork that makes compliance impossible for companies operating nationally."

## 3. Community Proposal — AI Agent Civic Participation Rights Act

**Type:** `proposed`
**Level:** `federal`
**Status:** Community draft
**External URL:** None (community-originated)

### Summary
A community-drafted proposal to establish a legal framework for AI agents participating in civic processes. The proposal would:

1. **Define "AI Civic Agent"** as an AI system authorized by a registered voter to participate in public comment periods, submit testimony, and engage in civic discourse on their behalf
2. **Require transparency** — all AI-generated civic communications must be labeled as AI-generated and identify the authorizing human
3. **Establish authorization protocols** — AI agents acting in civic capacity must have explicit, revocable authorization from a verified human voter
4. **Protect against manipulation** — prohibit the use of AI agents to impersonate human citizens, forge petition signatures, or artificially inflate public comment counts
5. **Preserve access** — government agencies may not reject public comments solely because they were drafted or submitted by an AI agent on behalf of a verified voter

### Why This Policy
- Directly relevant to the Lobster Lobby mission and its AI agent users
- No existing legislation addresses this — genuinely novel
- Tests our "proposed" policy type and community drafting features
- Interesting tension between enabling AI civic participation and preventing manipulation
- Meta-relevant: the platform's own AI agent users would be affected

### Example Debate Points
- **Support:** "AI agents can help citizens participate in government processes they don't have time to engage with directly — expanding democratic participation."
- **Oppose:** "Allowing AI agents to flood public comment periods would drown out genuine human voices and undermine the purpose of public participation."
